What is Tactical Advance Communication?

TAC is the next generation of communication tool for first responders. Historically, first responders have developed strategies for talking to emotionally distressed citizens).  Early communication strategies such as “verbal judo:  or “ask, tell, make”, or “feel, felt, found” are more effective than not having a strategy at all.  TAC improves on these earlier communication strategies to provide better tools for communicating to distressed citizens, which will make for safer environments for everyone.   This game is will train some of the verbal skills needed in the streets.

TAC is offered in appreciation of the heroes that risk their lives to help make our world a safer place.

 

Why does it works?

Part A:   When the general public interacts with first responders there is usually a power imbalance between the first responder a distressed citizen.  For example, the citizen might be need help in a situation, or might be afraid of being arrested from the police officer. In a situation where there is a power imbalance, the weaker person is usually afraid that they will not be listened to and respected by the more powerful person.   So, the question becomes, how can a first responder convey to the distressed citizen that they are being listened to and respected?

 

Part B:  We start with the assumptions that A.) all people have the same kinds of needs. Such as a need for respect, safety, peace, empathy, freedom, ....  B.) People do things to meet their needs. Everything a person does is to meet some need.    When you talk to a person about their needs, they usually feel listened to because you are talking to them about what is important to them at the that moment.   

 

Part A + Part B = A real-world strategy. TAC works because it gives first responders a tool to help them address the power imbalance while creating a connection to the citizen’s needs.   This approach has been tested in real-life encounters in the streets by police officers with great success.  This strategy is more effective in deescalating situations than other communication strategies because it gets to the core issues of the situation quickly and without blame.

 

 

How to do TAC in the streets?

1.) Start with a list of needs.

While we recognize that people have many needs, this list should handle most of the situations for a first responder.  Think of these needs words as bullets for your “communication gun.”

security, empathy, support, stability, safety, consideration, respect, understanding, shared reality, compassion, food/water, freedom, movement, choice, order, shelter, care, peace. 

 

2.) Try to make a needs-based connection

When talking to an emotionally elevated person at the scene ask about what they are needing using one of the words above.  For example, “Hey buddy, it looks like you are not getting the respect and safety that you want here?”

 

3.) Keep the conversation following up on their needs until the distressed citizen calms down. 

It is very tempting after an initial connection with a distressed citizen to quickly try to suggest a solution. (For example, the policeman might suggest, “We can take you to the hospital?” )  Distressing situations are uncomfortable, so most people are inclined to rush to fix things.  Instead of rushing to solutions, spending another few minutes to explore the needs will create a deeper connection in most situations.

 

Real world advice

  • TAC is unnatural to you.   It will feel strange to communicate with TAC at first.  Our DNA programs us for fight or flight in stressful situations, which is not useful for many stressful situations.  Just like practicing good habits for gun battles, the first responder will need to train to be able to use these communication tools under stressful environments.
  • People may not hear you the first time. Due to either previous experience with first responders or within their own families, people may not expect a person to ask about their needs.
  • This works for people who are under the influence of drugs or alcohol. Typically, people under the influence will be forgetful and you might need to go through the same conversation several times.
  • Nothing works ALL the time. Distressed citizens that are unable to discuss their needs may require the use of physical techniques comply with the law.
  • Be careful when the citizen responds to your question with, “What!?”.   In most cases they have heard you correctly, but are stalling to figure out what they are going to do.

 

Advanced topics and more info

Fake feelings and Needs – As mentioned above, TAC assumes all people have the same list of needs.  A need is defined as something that does not require another specific person/thing to fulfill it.   There is a fundamental belief that there are many different strategies possible to meet a need.

   An easy example is,

  • I have a need for food. (Correct, not specific, many foods can meet the need)
  • I have a need for chocolate (Incorrect, it is specific to chocolate)

  Harder examples:

  • I have a need love. Correct, not specific)
  • I need love from Joe Smith. (Incorrect, it is specific to Joe.)

   or

  • I need respect (Correct)
  • I need Joe Smith to show me some respect (incorrect).

 

The same is true for feelings.  TAC defined feels as an emotion that does not imply that another person has done something wrong. For example:

  • I feel sad. (Correct, this is simply a feeling)
  • I feel disrespected (Incorrect since it implies someone else disrespected her)
  • I feel lonely (Correct)
  • I feel abandoned or rejected (Incorrect since it implies someone else disrespected her)

 

What we don’t do and why we don’t do it.

  • We don’t ask what people what they think. If we ask what people think we usually get a whole bunch of opinions about whose fault it is.
  • We don’t spend much time asking about the distressed person’s feelings.
    • The TAC approach believes that feelings are the result of our met/unmet needs. (If your need for food is not met, then you feel hungry. 
    • We may also avoid talking about feelings by reframing the emotion for the distress citizen. For example, if a distressed citizen is very angry, we might say, “I can see that you are frustrated”, rather than saying “angry.” We can acknowledge the emotion (connection) and try to reframe it with citizen (leading) to create a calmer situation where needs can be discussed.
